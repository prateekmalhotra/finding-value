### 1) Network Effects (weight 18%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
The theme park business model lacks network effects. The value proposition for a guest does not increase as more guests attend; in fact, overcrowding can detract from the experience. There is no evidence of a user base that creates compounding value, and the company's offerings are self-contained entertainment experiences, not platforms.

**Evidence (1–3 bullets)**
*   The business model is based on attracting guests to physical locations for rides and entertainment, with no mechanism for user interactions to create additional value for other users. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The primary target demographics are "families and young people ages 12 through 24," who are attracted by rides and atmosphere, not by the number of other users in a network. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).

**Score:** 0 / 5.

### 2) Switching Costs (weight 15%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
Switching costs are low but not zero, primarily driven by geographic lock-in and seasonal pass programs. For regional customers, the closest Six Flags park is often the most convenient and lowest-cost full-scale amusement park option, creating a friction to switching to a competitor in a different metropolitan area. Season passes create a behavioral lock-in for a single operating season, encouraging repeat visitation and discouraging visits to competing parks during that period.

**Evidence (1–3 bullets)**
*   The company's parks are strategically located to "serve each of the top 10 designated market areas" in the U.S., reinforcing the advantage of proximity for a large customer base. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   Deferred revenue, which includes season pass and membership sales, was $308.3 million as of December 31, 2024, indicating a base of customers financially committed for the upcoming season. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).

**Score:** 2 / 5.

### 3) Cost Advantage / Unit Cost Leadership (weight 15%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
Six Flags has a limited cost advantage derived from the scale of the combined entity post-merger, which may provide some purchasing power for rides and supplies. However, the business has a very high fixed-cost structure for maintenance, staffing, and utilities that does not vary significantly with attendance, limiting margin benefits during periods of lower attendance. The company's "Project Accelerate" strategy explicitly targets "operating efficiencies that generate cost synergies," suggesting that cost advantages are an opportunity for improvement rather than a current, durable strength.

**Evidence (1–3 bullets)**
*   A significant portion of expenses are "relatively fixed," including costs for "full-time employees, maintenance, utilities, advertising and insurance," which do not vary significantly with attendance. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The company identifies "increased wage rates... higher insurance costs, and general inflation" as recent impacts on its principal costs and expenses. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).

**Score:** 2 / 5.

### 4) Intangible Assets / Brand / IP (weight 12%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
The company's primary intangible assets are its brand recognition and long-term, exclusive IP licensing agreements. The Six Flags brand is widely known in the regional theme park market, but it does not command a premium price like destination parks. The key advantage comes from exclusive theme park rights to Warner Bros. and DC Comics characters, which provide marketable and recognizable themes for rides and attractions that are difficult for regional competitors to replicate.

**Evidence (1–3 bullets)**
*   The company holds "exclusive long-term theme park usage rights in the U.S. (except for the Las Vegas metropolitan area and the state of Florida), Canada and Mexico to certain Warner Bros. and DC Comics animated characters." (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The company also holds "exclusive amusement and water park usage rights in the U.S. and Canada to the Peanuts comic strip characters." (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The value of the Six Flags trade name was preliminarily recorded at USD 850 million during the merger purchase price allocation, indicating significant brand value. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).

**Score:** 3 / 5.

### 5) Distribution & Efficient Scale (weight 10%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
This is one of the company's strongest advantages. Six Flags operates a geographically diversified portfolio of 42 parks, many of which are located near major metropolitan areas, creating localized monopolies or duopolies. The immense cost and difficulty of acquiring large tracts of land and building a new theme park make it prohibitively expensive for a new competitor to enter one of its established markets.

**Evidence (1–3 bullets)**
*   The company is North America's "largest regional amusement park operator with 27 amusement parks, 15 separately gated water parks and nine resort properties" across the U.S., Canada, and Mexico. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The park portfolio includes significant owned acreage, such as 2,107 acres in Jackson, NJ (Great Adventure) and 255 acres in Valencia, CA (Magic Mountain), which are difficult-to-replicate assets in strategic locations. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   Principal competitive factors include "proximity to metropolitan areas," highlighting the advantage of its existing park locations. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).

**Score:** 4 / 5.

### 8) Regulatory / Legal Barriers (weight 6%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
Six Flags faces significant regulatory hurdles related to ride safety, environmental laws, and zoning, which create a moderate barrier to entry for new competitors. While the company does not hold unique, non-replicable government licenses, the cumulative burden of compliance is substantial and costly for a de novo entrant. These barriers protect incumbent operators like Six Flags from new competition but do not prevent existing competitors from operating.

### 9) Capital Intensity & Asset Specificity (weight 4%)
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences)**
This represents a powerful barrier to entry and a core component of the company's moat. Theme parks are exceptionally capital-intensive to build and maintain, with highly specific assets (roller coasters, themed structures) that have little alternative use. The immense, multi-hundred-million-dollar upfront investment and long lead times required to construct a competing park serves as a strong deterrent to new entrants in Six Flags' established markets.

**Evidence (1–3 bullets)**
*   The company's net property and equipment is valued at approximately USD 4.3 billion, demonstrating the massive capital asset base. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The company plans for capital expenditures of between USD 475 million and USD 500 million in 2025 alone to add new attractions and maintain existing ones, highlighting the high ongoing investment required. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
*   The business requires "specialized manufacturing" for many of its rides and attractions, indicating asset specificity. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).

**Score:** 5 / 5.

### Moat Duration Justification
The company's moat is primarily based on its physical assets and geographic locations, which are highly durable and difficult to replicate, suggesting a long potential duration. However, this is offset by the finite nature of its key IP licenses with Warner Bros./DC Comics. The durability estimate is therefore constrained to a 5–10 year horizon, reflecting the risk, however remote, of non-renewal of these critical branding agreements and the constant need for capital investment to maintain appeal.

### Top 3 Moat Erosion Scenarios
1.  **Loss of Key IP Licenses:** The termination of the Warner Bros./DC Comics license would remove a key differentiator for new attractions and branding, making the parks more generic and vulnerable to competition from other regional players. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
2.  **Sustained Shift in Consumer Discretionary Spending:** A long-term economic downturn or shift in consumer preference away from theme parks towards in-home or other forms of entertainment could reduce attendance, forcing the company to compete more heavily on price and eroding margins. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
3.  **Major Safety Incident:** A catastrophic accident at a Six Flags park or a competitor's park could severely damage public perception of safety in the industry, leading to a sharp, sustained decline in attendance and increased regulatory scrutiny. (Form 10-K, Feb 21, 2025).
