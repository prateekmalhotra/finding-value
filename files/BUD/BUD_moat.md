### **1) Network Effects**
**Reasoning:** Network effects are negligible in the beer industry. A customer's choice of beer is not directly influenced by the number of other people drinking the same brand in a way that increases the product's utility. While brand popularity can create social proof, it is not a true network effect where the service improves with each additional user.
**Evidence:**
*   The business model is based on producing and selling a physical product, where value is intrinsic to the product itself, not the number of users consuming it. This is an inference based on the nature of the consumer-packaged goods industry.
**Score:** 1 / 5

### **2) Switching Costs**
**Reasoning:** Switching costs for consumers are exceptionally low, as they can easily choose a different beer brand on any given occasion. For retailers and distributors, switching costs are moderately higher due to established relationships, contracts, and logistical integrations with a major supplier like AB InBev. However, these are not insurmountable barriers.
**Evidence:**
*   The company focuses on brand loyalty and marketing to retain consumers, which implicitly acknowledges the low barriers to switching for the end-user. This is an inference based on standard industry practice.
**Score:** 2 / 5

### **3) Cost Advantage / Unit Cost Leadership**
**Reasoning:** AB InBev's massive global scale affords it significant economies of scale in sourcing raw materials (barley, hops), manufacturing, and logistics. This allows the company to produce beer at a lower per-unit cost than nearly any competitor, supporting strong gross margins and competitive pricing.
**Evidence:**
*   The company's global footprint and volume leadership create significant purchasing power with agricultural suppliers and packaging manufacturers, driving down input costs. This is a well-established principle of economies of scale in this industry.
**Score:** 4 / 5

### **4) Intangible Assets / Brand / IP**
**Reasoning:** The company commands a portfolio of globally recognized brands, including Budweiser, Stella Artois, and Corona, which are among the most valuable beer brands in the world. This brand equity allows for premium pricing and creates a strong consumer pull. While individual recipes are not protected by patents, the trademarks are immensely valuable and perpetual.
**Evidence:**
*   AB InBev's portfolio includes "three global brands, Budweiser, Stella Artois and Corona," alongside numerous other billion-dollar brands that command consumer loyalty and premium price points. (AB InBev FY 2024 Results, Feb 26, 2025)
*   The company's stated purpose is to "Dream Big to Create a Future with More Cheers," which is built upon a brand portfolio with a heritage dating back centuries.
**Score:** 4 / 5

### **5) Distribution & Efficient Scale**
**Reasoning:** AB InBev possesses one of the most extensive and efficient distribution networks in the world, reaching tens of millions of points of sale. This network, built over decades, represents a formidable barrier to entry, as a new competitor would need to invest billions and many years to replicate such a logistical footprint. The scale is so large in many markets that it creates a natural duopoly or oligopoly, making it unprofitable for smaller players to compete on a national level.
**Evidence:**
*   The company operates globally with a presence in dozens of countries and an extensive portfolio of over 500 beer and soft drink brands, requiring a vast and complex distribution system.
**Score:** 5 / 5

### **6) Data Advantage**
**Reasoning:** AB InBev is increasingly leveraging data from its direct-to-consumer platforms and sales channels to understand consumer preferences and optimize its supply chain. Through platforms like BEES, it gathers data from its B2B customers. While this is a growing advantage, it is not yet a deep, proprietary moat compared to tech companies.
**Evidence:**
*   The company's strategic initiatives include leveraging technology and data analytics to improve operational efficiency and better meet consumer needs. This is an inference from management discussions on digital transformation.
**Score:** 2 / 5

### **7) Ecosystem / Platform Effects**
**Reasoning:** The company is building a B2B ecosystem with its BEES platform, which provides retailers with purchasing, logistics, and management tools. However, this is still primarily a one-sided ecosystem focused on streamlining AB InBev's sales channels. It does not yet have the strong, multi-sided platform effects seen in technology or financial services industries.
**Evidence:**
*   Management has highlighted the growth of the BEES platform as a key part of its digital transformation strategy, aiming to create more value for its retail partners. (AB InBev FY 2024 Results, Feb 26, 2025)
**Score:** 1 / 5

### **8) Regulatory / Legal Barriers**
**Reasoning:** The alcoholic beverage industry is heavily regulated, with complex licensing laws that vary by country and state, creating a significant barrier to entry. AB InBev's established legal and compliance infrastructure is a key asset. Recent legislation in key markets can also be favorable, creating advantages against foreign competitors.
**Evidence:**
*   Missouri's "American Beer Act" lowers the state excise tax for domestic brewers like Anheuser-Busch, while imported malt beverages will pay a higher price, creating a direct, government-enforced competitive advantage in that state. (Seeking Alpha, August 19, 2025)
**Score:** 3 / 5

### **9) Capital Intensity & Asset Specificity**
**Reasoning:** The brewing and distribution industry is highly capital intensive, requiring massive investments in breweries, supply chain logistics, and vehicle fleets. These assets are highly specific and cannot be easily repurposed, creating a high barrier to entry and deterring potential competitors who would face significant sunk costs.
**Evidence:**
*   AB InBev is investing $15M in its St. Louis brewery alone to increase supply chain efficiencies, illustrating the ongoing capital required to maintain and improve its large-scale, specific assets. (Seeking Alpha, August 19, 2025)
*   The company also recently invested $9M in its Baldwinsville, NY facility to expand capacity. (Seeking Alpha, August 19, 2025)
**Score:** 4 / 5

### **10) Customer Contracts & Revenue Visibility**
**Reasoning:** A significant portion of AB InBev's revenue comes from non-contracted sales to consumers through retailers. While there are agreements with distributors and large retailers, these do not provide the long-term, recurring revenue visibility seen in enterprise software or subscription-based businesses.
**Evidence:**
*   The business model is transactional, based on purchase orders from distributors and retailers, which can fluctuate based on consumer demand and seasonality. This is an inference based on the nature of the business.
**Score:** 1 / 5

### **Moat Duration Justification**
The primary sources of AB InBev's moat are its cost advantages from scale, its powerful brands, and its unparalleled distribution network. These advantages are highly durable and difficult to replicate, suggesting a long-term competitive advantage. The core brands are timeless, and the physical infrastructure for brewing and distribution would require decades of sustained, multi-billion dollar investment to challenge globally. Therefore, the estimated moat duration is approximately 10–12 years.

### **Top 3 Moat Erosion Scenarios**
1.  **Shift in Consumer Tastes:** A significant and sustained consumer shift away from beer towards spirits, wine, or non-alcoholic beverages could erode the value of AB InBev's core brands and production assets.
2.  **Rise of Craft/Hyper-Local Brands:** The continued growth and fragmentation of the market due to the rise of craft breweries could slowly chip away at the market share of macro-brands, weakening AB InBev's pricing power and volumes.
3.  **Regulatory Intervention:** Antitrust actions in key markets could force divestitures of brands or distribution assets, directly weakening the company's scale advantages and efficient scale moat.

### **KPIs to Monitor**
*   **Organic Volume Growth:** A decline indicates eroding brand loyalty or market share loss.
*   **Gross Margin vs. Peers:** Compression could signal a loss of cost advantage.
*   **Brand-Level Market Share:** Monitor the share of Budweiser, Corona, and Stella Artois in their key markets.
*   **BEES Platform Adoption Rate:** Growth in B2B platform users is key to their data and ecosystem strategy.
*   **Capital Expenditures as % of Revenue:** A significant increase may indicate higher costs to maintain their asset base.
*   **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC):** A long-term decline suggests a deterioration of the competitive position.

### **Short Verdict Paragraph**
Anheuser-Busch InBev possesses a moderate moat built on the pillars of cost advantage, intangible brand assets, and a vast distribution network. Its global scale provides significant economies in production and sourcing that are difficult for any competitor to match. This is complemented by a portfolio of iconic brands that command consumer loyalty and premium prices. While the company faces low consumer switching costs and minimal network effects, its entrenched physical infrastructure and regulatory know-how create a durable competitive advantage. The moat rating would be higher if the company had more direct control over its customers, stronger contractual revenue, or a more developed data moat.
