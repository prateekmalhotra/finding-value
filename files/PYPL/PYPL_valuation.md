The original valuation was well-structured and followed a sound methodology. However, several key issues required correction and refinement to bring the assumptions closer to current market realities and best practices.

**Key Issues Identified in the Original Analysis:**

1.  **Outdated & Fictional Data**: The analysis used a future date (August 2025) and corresponding hypothetical financial data. A valuation must be grounded in the most recent, publicly available information.
2.  **WACC Calculation**: The Cost of Debt was calculated using the *effective interest rate* (Interest Expense / Debt), which reflects past borrowing costs. The correct approach uses the *marginal cost of debt*, reflecting current market rates for a company with PayPal's credit profile. Additionally, the Equity Risk Premium (ERP) was slightly low compared to current academic and practitioner consensus.
3.  **Terminal Value Discrepancy**: The original analysis correctly identified that its Gordon Growth Method produced an unrealistically low implied exit multiple (6.3x). However, it then selected a 10.0x multiple with limited justification. A more robust analysis requires a stronger rationale for the chosen multiple, grounded in industry comparables and the company's future state.
4.  **Operating Margin Assumption**: Holding the margin perfectly flat is a safe but potentially inaccurate assumption, especially with a new management team heavily focused on improving operating leverage and "profitable growth."

The following revised valuation corrects these issues by using current data, refining the WACC and terminal value calculations, and incorporating more nuanced assumptions.

### **Part 2: Analyst's Revised Valuation (Realistic Base-Case)**

**C) FORMULATE ASSUMPTIONS (5 YEARS)**

6.  **Rationale**: The market's implied 6.5% growth is slightly below current analyst consensus (7-8% for the next two years). My base case will align with this consensus initially, before tapering, reflecting both management's initiatives and persistent competitive pressures.

7.  **Revenue Growth (Years 1-5)**: I will use **7.5%** growth for Year 1, tapering down by 0.5% each year to **5.5%** in Year 5. This reflects a modest re-acceleration from new product rollouts followed by maturation.

8.  **Operating Margin**: The TTM margin is 16.32%. With new management's intense focus on efficiency and profitable growth, a flat margin is too conservative. I will model a modest expansion of 20 basis points (0.20%) per year, reaching **17.32%** in Year 5. This reflects successful cost-cutting and a better product mix without being overly optimistic.

9.  **Taxes**: The TTM effective tax rate is around 10%, but this is influenced by one-time items. The average over the last three fiscal years is closer to 19%. **F) TERMINAL VALUE**

17. **Rationale**: The original model's key weakness was justifying the terminal value. Using a single method can be misleading. Here, we'll use a primary method (Exit Multiple) and cross-check it with the Gordon Growth method to ensure our assumptions are realistic. The Exit Multiple approach is often more appropriate for companies like PayPal, where long-term returns on capital may not significantly exceed WACC.

18. **Exit Multiple Method**:
    *   Year 5 EBITDA = Year 5 EBIT + Year 5 D&A = $7,115M + $1,767M = $8,882M.
    *   **Justification**: Mature payment processors like Visa (V) and Mastercard (MA) trade at 18-22x EV/EBITDA due to their network effects and higher margins. Slower-growth legacy tech companies trade from 8-12x. In five years, a mature PayPal will likely sit between these two poles. A terminal multiple of **12.0x EV/EBITDA** is a realistic assumption. It's a premium to legacy tech, acknowledging PayPal's brand and scale, but a significant discount to the premier payment rails.
    *   **Terminal Value = 12.0 * $8,882M = $106,584 million.**

19. **Gordon Growth Cross-Check**:
    *   Terminal Growth Rate (g): 3.0% (Slightly higher to reflect long-term nominal GDP growth).
    *   Terminal Value (GG) = (FCFF Year 5 * (1 + g)) / (WACC - g) = ($6,016 * 1.03) / (11.37% - 3.0%) = $6,196 / 8.37% = $74,026 million.
    *   This implies a terminal EV/EBITDA multiple of $74,026M / $8,882M = **8.3x**. While the updated WACC makes this more reasonable than the original's 6.3x, it still appears too conservative for a market leader. This reinforces the decision to use the more optimistic, but well-justified, Exit Multiple method as the primary valuation driver.
